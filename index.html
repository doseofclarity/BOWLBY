Overview & Core Concepts

John Bowlby (1907–1990) – British psychiatrist who developed Attachment Theory, emphasizing that attachment is a biologically instinctive bond that ensures infant survival. The quality of early attachment affects lifelong emotional, social, and cognitive development.

🔑 Key Concepts
Secure base, Internal working model, Separation anxiety, Maternal deprivation.
📊 Mnemonic (4 phases):
"P.A.C.R." – Pre-attachment, Attachment-in-the-making, Clear-cut, Reciprocal.
Key concept: Attachment is not just a behavioral connection; it is an emotional bond that provides a sense of security.

4 Phases of Attachment (Bowlby)

PhaseAgeDescription
Pre-attachmentBirth – 6 weeksInfant uses reflexes (crying, grasping, smiling) to attract caregivers; no discrimination.
Attachment-in-the-making6 weeks – 6–8 monthsBegins to prefer familiar caregivers; trust develops; separation protest minimal.
Clear-cut attachment6–8 months – 18–24 monthsStrong, specific attachment; separation anxiety and stranger anxiety peak; caregiver as secure base.
Formation of reciprocal relationship18–24 months +Child understands caregiver's comings and goings; can tolerate separations; mutual relationship.

Separation Anxiety (Protest, Despair, Detachment)

Bowlby's 3 stages of separation behavior:

1️⃣ Protest 😭
Child cries, screams, searches for parent; may reject substitute caregivers.
2️⃣ Despair 😔
Child becomes quiet, withdrawn, hopeless, apathetic; still misses parent.
3️⃣ Detachment 😶
Appears recovered but emotionally distant; may ignore or avoid parent upon return.
⚠️ Nursing alert: Prolonged separation without consistent care can lead to lasting emotional damage and insecure attachment.

Internal Working Model & Maternal Deprivation

🧠 Internal Working Model (IWM)
Mental blueprint formed from early attachment experiences. Shapes expectations about self, others, and relationships. Secure attachment → positive IWM (trust, self-worth). Insecure attachment → negative IWM (anxiety, avoidance).
⚠️ Maternal Deprivation Hypothesis
Bowlby proposed that continuous care from a primary attachment figure during the first 2–3 years is essential. Prolonged deprivation leads to emotional coldness, delinquency, low IQ, and poor social relationships. Study: "44 Juvenile Thieves" showed high rates of early separations.
Critical period: First 2–3 years of life for attachment formation.

Nursing Implications & Interventions

  • Promote early bonding: Encourage skin-to-skin contact (kangaroo care), rooming‑in, breastfeeding, and parental presence during procedures.
  • Prevent unnecessary separation: Allow parents to stay during hospitalization; explain benefits of parental involvement.
  • Support responsive caregiving: Teach parents to recognize and respond to infant cues (crying, cooing, eye contact).
  • Identify attachment problems: Observe parent‑infant interaction, eye contact, holding, vocalization; refer if signs of neglect or emotional detachment.
  • Provide anticipatory guidance: Educate about normal separation anxiety (8 months) and how to ease transitions.
  • Support foster/adoptive families: Help parents understand attachment challenges and promote consistency and warmth.
Nursing mnemonic "C.A.R.E.": C – Consistent caregiver, A – Attentive to cues, R – Responsive interaction, E – Encourage bonding.
